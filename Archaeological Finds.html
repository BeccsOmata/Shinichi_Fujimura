Archaeological Finds


    
Paleolithic Period and Jmon Era History

     Before discussing the archaeological finds that Shinichi Fujimura "discovered", I find it important to discuss the difference between the Japanese Paleolithic period and the Jmon Era.

     The Japanese Paleolithic period generally indicates a time of human habitation within an area that was before 10,000 BCE, with a starting date of around 40,000 BCE. The creation of pottery was not yet established, and people were living nomadic lifestyles, as well as in small campsites towards the later period (Keally, 2002).

     The population at this time relied on pebble and flake tools, backed tools, microliths, as well as bifacial projectile points. A big factor in determining if tools originated from this era, is the volcanic eruption that occurred within Japan sometime around 22-24,000 years ago, as a layer of distinguishable volcanic ash named Aira-Tanzawa (AT) tephra covered the majority of the country, making it possible to discern artifacts from before or after AT (Keally, 2002).


     Comparatively, the Jmon Era occurred between 14,000-300 BCE. These peoples relied on hunting and gathering, and developed pottery. The transition between the Late Paleolithic and Early Jmon era is very gradual, with the transition starting when microblades and pottery emerge. Subsequently, it is believed that Jmon villages were set up with thatched roofs and an open plaza, with sometimes containing 5 to 10 members (Keally, 2009).


Shinichi Fujimura's Take


     Shinichi Fujimura claimed to have found Paleolithic stone tools at Kamitakamori Ruins, that of which would have dated back evidence of the earliest human habitations within Japan being 600 millennia ago, rather than the previously estimated 30 millennia ago. One would think that these finds were often concerning, as the context in which they were found did not match with the artifacts, themselves, but it was quite common for abnormal finds to occur, thus why Fujimura's findings were so easily accepted (Salmon, 2020).


Fujimura's Stone Tools

     With the story breaking of Fujimura's artifact findings being that of hoaxes, it was then made known that Fujimura would displace previously found stone tools and Jmon artifacts from various Jmon Era and Early Paleolithic sites, to then place them at the locations he was excavating at. This was most commonly and infamously done at the so-called Kamitakamori Ruins, among other sites, like the Sshin Fudzaka site in Hokkaido (Hudson, 2005).
How Did He Get So Far?

     After WWII, Japanese archaeology was seen as a means of defining Japanese cultural and historical processes. With the denouncement of the Japanese emperor's royal bloodline, Japan questioned its own origin and place within evolutionary history, rather then relying on Japanese mythology (Hudson, 2005).

     Amateur archaeologists were seen as a positive contribution in uncovering Japan's historic evolutionary past, and it were topics like the Early Paleolithic time period that Japan saw as exciting research. Additionally, there was a lack in the scholarly debate of thorough scientific analyses of these Early Paleolithic artifacts and sites (Hudson, 2005).

     With Japanese archaeology not being centered around academia, and more-so focused on an individual gaining recognition for astounding finds, it was considered a personal insult if someone were to claim fraud against another professional within the field (Salmon, 2020).

     Regarding Fujimura, it was rather difficult to detect if an artifact had been planted by him or not, as the tools that he "recovered" were ancient and found in optimal locations of the site he was excavating. Subsequently, he often executed his mission of planting artifacts early in the morning, and due to him being held with high esteem within the archaeological field, many of the inconsistencies found with his finds were ignored (Salmon, 2020).

     It is due to the factors of high Japanese social and cultural expectations, the amount of fame and recognition that Fujimura and his affiliated institutions would garner, the non-academic interpretations of archaeological finds, and the popularization of archaeology by the media on archaeological discoveries, that one could discern how and why Fujimura was able to extend the length of his hoaxes for as long as he did (Hudson, 2005).
