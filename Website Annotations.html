Website Annotations



 1) The Stone Age Discoveries of Sinichi Fujimura, (2017)

~ Link - https://www.pressreader.com/uk/lets-talk/20170301/283025464380528
~ Description - This website gives a very brief, but good, explanation on the rise and fall of Fujimura. The tone of it seems very generous at first, praising Fujimura for the work that he did, however, as soon as it details of his hoaxes, all of the prestigious words are omitted, and a more rough tone is adopted. It describes his quick rise to fame with his find of the 40,000 year old stoneware, to his decline with Mainichi Shimbun's photographic leak of Fujimura planting artifacts. It ends with detailing of his banishment from the Japanese Archaeological Association (JAA), and his dismissal from the Paleolithic Institute.


2) Archaeologist Exposed as Fraud, (2000)
~ Link - http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/asia-pacific/1008051.stm
~ Description - A description of Fujimura being caught on camera is discussed, yet again. This time, a small excerpt on Fujimura apologizing is provided, with him stating that he "...fell a victim to temptation". The website further elaborates on how Fujimura has planted numerous fake artifacts within his career, and how his reputation has ultimately crumbled due to his fake stunts. They also recognize that Japan's Paleolithic era may be forced to go under review as a result of Fujimura's efforts. The released images from the Mainichi Shimbun are also present on the site.


3) Faking the Past: When Archaeologists Commit Fraud, (2014)
~ Link - http://www.anonymousswisscollector.com/2014/10/faking-the-past-when-archaeologists-manufacture-illicit-antiquities.html
~ Description - The Anonymous Swiss Collector elaborates on several archaeological frauds on this website, with Shinichi Fujimura being one of them. They start right off the bat by saying that Fujimura was not an archaeologist, rather, he was an archaeology enthusiast. The author talks about how the geology in which Fujimura's artifacts were found, did not support Fujimura's archaeology. They also bring to light the fact that Japan's academic environment could be partially to blame, as there was a lack in the information shared among individuals and institutions. This piece also ends in the downfall of his career and removal of his archaeological finds from museums. A few articles are cited in this website, so I would venture to say that this is a rather credible source.


4) Shinichi Fujimura, (2018)
~ Link - https://alchetron.com/Shinichi-Fujimura
~ Description - The author of this website does a great job of visualizing the story of Shinichi Fujimura. They decided to include a picture of the stone tools that Fujimura "recovered" from an archaeological site that he visited, a map to indicate the location in which Fujimura executed his excavations, pictures from the Mainichi Shimbun, and a photo of Fujimura at an archaeological site with other archaeologists. Again, it is stated that some geologists and anthropologists questioned his work, as his findings did not possess consistency with the geologic evaluation of the site in correlation with the artifacts. Unlike the previous websites, this one describes Mitsuo Kagawa as possibly having been involved in the hoax, along with his suicide.


5) Faked Digs Put Archaeologists on Defensive by Taiga Uranaka, (2001)
~ Link - https://www.trussel.com/prehist/news240.htm
~ Description - Here, the site starts off with a very shocked tone, and discusses things done by various institutions as a result of the claims on Fujimura. For example, they discuss how the Cultural Agency was advised to re-examine the sites Fujimura visited, and how the central government of Japan offered to pay for half the costs of the projects that Fujimura staged, which were originally paid for by tax-payers. It seems that at the time of this article's publication, that official statements about Fujimura had yet to be made, as the author talks about how they are uncertain how greatly Fujimura will impact the Japanese archaeological world, and concludes with how those who once supported him, no longer did.


6) The Amateur Who Upended East Asian Archaeology, (No year given)
~ Link - https://historycollection.com/the-baron-of-arizona-and-other-historic-hucksters/9/
~ Description - It is discussed the importance of archaeology within Japanese cultural and social contexts. Thus, when Fujimura introduced his archaeological finds, his discoveries made headlines and proved that humans had existed longer than originally anticipated within Japan. Unlike the other websites, this one attributes just how much Japan liked to set themselves apart from China, as Japan has many cultural influences from China. It were discoveries like Fujimura's that displayed the uniqueness of Japan from China, that of which made him more likeable among the Japanese masses. The next page of the website briefly describes the downfall of his hoaxes. No articles are cited within this website.


7) Were Artifacts Planted at Japanese Site? by Dennis Normile, (2001)
~ Link - https://www.sciencemag.org/news/2001/11/were-artifacts-planted-japanese-site
~ Description - The author here, instead of focusing too much on Fujimura, decides to shed light on Mitsuo Kagawa, the presumed accomplice in the fabrication of artifacts with Fujimura. It is here where the author briefly describes how Kagawa led excavations in 1961 and 1962 which yielded animal bones and stone artifacts that were said to have been dated back 10,000 years or more, and how these finds were proven to be controversial as radiocarbon dating showed that these artifacts were no more than 700 years old. Kagawa is not related to Fujimura's incidents, however, a local news paper attempted to tie the two together, which ultimately led to Kagawa committing suicide. Kagawa believed that the artifacts found at the site he was working on needed to be reexamined anyway, but could not stomach to be called hoax. No citations are present for this insert.


8) The Stone Age Discoveries of Shinichi Fujimura, (2003)
~ Link - http://hoaxes.org/archive/permalink/the_stone_age_discoveries_of_shinichi_fujimura
~ Description - This page provides an interesting insight (and brief mention) on how Fujimura's finds gained popularity within the town of Tsukidate, where tourism trade was booming from the volume of people that the archaeological site within the town attracted; a site that Fujimura was working within. Later written within the site is the all too familiar explanation on the downfall of Fujimura's career with the publishing of the Mainichi Shimbun's scandalous pictures of Fujimura, his admittance to his crimes, the shock that resonated throughout his peers, and the questioning of how Fujimura was able to get away with his hoax for as long as he did. This site says the reasoning as to why he was able to successfully fool people, was that it was difficult to date stone implements, as they can only be identified through strata where they were found. The article concludes that the Fujimura incident is comparative to another Japanese hoax, Nobuo Naora. Links and references are given at the end.


9) Digging Out of the Scandal by Peter Bleed, (2000)
~ Link - http://www.t-net.ne.jp/~keally/Hoax/keally-bleed2.html
~ Description - Bleed sort of treats this post as a personal account, as well as thorough evaluation on the Fujimura incident. Here, Bleed describes having gone on archaeological evaluations with Fujimura, specifically when they both visited the excavations at the Nagaone site in Chichibu, where there were exposed pits with seemingly nothing in them, and being undoubtedly intact. It was then that Fujimura unearthed stone shards from the pits, and to Bleed's perception, he could not have guessed that said fragments were inauthentic to the site. He then describes how Tom Keally's accusation of Fujimura's findings to be suspicious were not taken seriously, but then looks retrospectively on that dismissal and supports Keally's claim; that Fujimura was falsely reporting artifacts, and how this has greatly affected Japanese archaeology in very serious ways. Bleed then ends with a plea in advocating for further research in the sites of Early and Middle Paleolithic sites in Japan.


10) Shinichi Fujimura by Grahame Johnston, (2017)
~ Link - http://www.archaeologyexpert.co.uk/shinichifujiama.html
~ Description - Johnston illuminates Japan's fascination with archaeology as a very popular science, with even entire sections of bookstores being devoted to archaeological happenings. They also detail of the tourism that Tsukidate acquired after having been a hot spot location for archaeological finds by Fujimura. The late discovery in Fujimura's trickery is, again, deemed because of how difficult it is to date lithic objects. The author concludes with Fujimura's dismissal from archaeology, the removal of him from Japanese text books, scientific books, and any archaeological texts. Johnston then informs of Japan not having been hoaxed this extensively since Nobuo Naora, where the amateur archaeologist claimed that he found the missing link to humanity, by unearthing a primitive hipbone. To others' avail, it was simply a lie. No citations are given at the end of this website.
