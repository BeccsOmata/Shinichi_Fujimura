History of Fujimura



     Shinichi Fujimura, a Japanese man who was then working for an electric-gadget company in his late 40's, had only obtained his high school degree as his highest form of education; he had no formal schooling in the methods and practices of archaeology and no professional experience, either. It was then, in 1972, that he decided to independently teach himself archaeology, and in 1981, he discovered significant stoneware that dated back 40 millennia, thus pushing back Japan's previous ancient history by 10 millennia. These types of finds were primarily discovered in the Japanese Paleolithic sites of the Zazaragi and Kamitakamori Ruins of Miyagi Prefecture (Salmon, 2020).

    After this success, Fujimura went on to examine over 180 other archaeological sites, with priority in evaluating sites that were classified as Paleolithic (Romey, 2001). It were at these sites, that he discovered more stone tools and Jmon Period artifacts, which, as stated before, seriously altered previously calculated Japanese archaeological records. With consistent impressive finds, Fujimura was coined the name "Hand of God" and "God's Hands". These finds were remarkable, and as such, his work was taught in Japanese school textbooks and widely praised between his peers and other archaeological institutions of Japan (Salmon, 2020).

     Many of his colleagues and fellow archaeologists were in support of his findings, however, there were a handful that had doubt against his work. Two individuals who contended Fujimura's research were Charles Keally and Toshiki Takeoka, both renowned archaeologists from different Japanese universities (Salmon, 2020). Due in part to Takeoka's concerns for Fujimura's authenticity, Takeoka notified the Mainichi Shimbun, a Japanese newspaper, who then set out for their journalists to track Fujimura for six months in an attempt to catch him performing suspicious acts within the archaeological sites Fujimura was researching at (Salmon, 2020).

     It was then in 2001 that Fujimura was exposed by the Mainichi Shimbun, where journalists of this newspaper released photo evidence of Fujimura burying Jmon artifacts in the soil at the Kamitakamori ruins. After discovering Fujimura's hoax, he was then investigated more, to which it was revealed that he acquired stolen artifacts from various Jmon Era sites in the Thoku region of Japan (Hudson, 2005). Fujimura's discrepancies would come to be known as the Japanese Paleolithic Hoax.


     Though those who yielded some sort of skepticism towards Fujimura's findings were few and far between, those who did question his research to be authentic were often hushed, as doubting Fujimura's authenticity was not a universal act, seen as personal attacks, and not considered into scientific conversations. Furthermore, it were institutions that gained positively from his findings that disregarded the inconsistencies, which is part of the reason why Fujimura was able to execute his mission of fraudulent artifact misplacement to the extent that he did (Hudson, 2005).
 
     Subsequently, in 2001, a Japanese magazine claimed that Fujimura did not act alone, and that Mituo Kagawa, a Japanese professor and archeologist at Beppu University, aided him in his deployment of the artifacts. This statement was mainly due in part to Kagawa finding artifacts at certain sites he was working on, that of which did not match with the context they were found in. This statement greatly affected Kagawa, so much so to the point of him committing suicide, leaving a detailed note that described his innocence in the matter of the Japanese Paleolithic Hoax. It is believed that Kagawa did not assist in Fujimura's hoaxes, as Kagawa had consistently stated that even his own findings needed to be reexamined (Normile, 2001).

     Prior to Fujimura being reprimanded, his findings and research were deeply impressionable to the foundational scientific understandings of Japanese Paleolithic analyses. Following the official announcement of his falsities in 2003 by the Japanese Archaeological Association (JAA), Fujimura was then expelled from both the JAA and from his job as chairman at the Tohoku Institute (Romey, 2001).

     To this day, it is unknown why Fujimura enacted upon his fabrications of Jmon artifacts and stone tools into Japanese Paleolithic excavation sites, however, it is speculated that through the social importance given to Japanese archaeology, that this pertinent social element could have been the cause for Fujimura to continue his implementing hoaxes across Japanese archaeological sites. Since his incident of the Japanese Paleolithic Hoax, he has undergone hospital treatment for mental illness, and has recovered from this. Lastly, with the JAA's recanting of Fujimura's findings, his work has since been taken out of school books, as the very mention of Fujimura's scientific hoxes yields an undoubtedly disappointed ring to it (Hudson, 2005).
